Why I'm Building Accrue

The agent model already exists. It just needs better rails.

Four years ago today, I wrote a post called "Accrue's Big Day!". After five months of beta testing and talking to early users, we'd finally launched. A thousand users. $150k AUM. Clinton, Adesuwa, and I were ready for what came next. Or so we thought. The problem we were solving was simple: make it easy for anyone to invest in crypto and stocks using dollar-cost averaging. Pick an asset, set an amount, and let the app handle the rest.

For a while, it worked. Then the bear market hit and everything stopped. Sign ups dried up, deposits vanished, and we pivoted to dollar savings, thinking stability would be enough. It wasn't. We were running out of runway and starting to make hard decisions just to survive.

Then a user in Ghana used one of our features to send money to her aunt in Nigeria. That wasn't what we built it for, but it made us pay attention. We followed that thread, and within seven months, cross-border payments went from less than 5% of our revenue to over 70%. Adesuwa wrote about this journey in detail, but the short version is: we almost didn't make it, and our users showed us what to build next.

Africa Moves a Lot of Money. The Problem is How.

Remittances to Africa hit nearly $100 billion in 2024, according to the World Bank. Factor in B2B trade and other cross-border flows, and Oui Capital estimates the total market at $329 billion. But 75% of that money moves through informal channels: Cash in envelopes. Bus drivers carrying bundles across borders. Family networks.

Why? Because formal transfers cost 8% on average, the highest rate in the world. And even when people use traditional channels, it means taking time off work to sit in a Western Union or bank queue. Add currency conversion losses, and you're looking at billions of dollars lost yearly to fees and friction.

That's the problem we're solving now.

Accrue is a consumer app for saving and moving money across Africa. Anyone in Africa can save in USD stablecoins and send and receive payments instantly. For millions facing volatile local currencies, stablecoins aren't speculation. They're the safest store of value available. And for those wondering why not just buy dollars from a bank: across much of the continent, you can't. Dollar shortages have become endemic in countries like Nigeria, Kenya, Egypt, Ghana, and Zambia. People trying to access foreign currency face liquidity constraints, long queues, and outright rejections. Stablecoins are how they access dollars when the banking system can't provide them.

And yes, you can still DCA into your favorite assets. That feature that started the company is still there. It's just part of something bigger now.

Cashramp is infrastructure. Think M-Pesa on stablecoin rails. It's a stablecoin agent network across over a dozen African countries. A user sends local currency to an agent via their preferred payment method, and receives stablecoin equivalent displayed as USD in the app. The conversion is automatic. The user never touches crypto. Their local currency simply gains the superpowers of programmable money.

Cashramp is the engine that powers Accrue. It's how Accrue users deposit and withdraw in local currency. It's how they send money to loved ones in other countries. To us, Accrue is a proof of concept, a way to show potential Cashramp customers what can be built on a continent-wide agent network.

When that money needs to cross a border, say from Ghana to Nigeria, the Ghanaian user sends cedis to a Cashramp agent, receives USD (stablecoins) at the prevailing market rate, and another agent in Nigeria converts it to naira and deposits it into the recipient's bank account. A 1% fee, capped at $2. One-minute settlement. Frictionless.

Read this brilliant article by Clinton about the Cashramp Agent Network.

Four Years of Work

Our payment success rate is 98%. Revenue has grown steadily quarter over quarter, mostly from organic demand. Over half of cross-border users come back at least three more times.

We've done this with a small, focused team embedded in the markets we serve. Agents with smartphones and a protocol that handles the rest.

The growth isn't hype-driven. It's utility-driven. People use Accrue & Cashramp because they solve real problems. A trader in Senegal can pay a supplier in Cte d'Ivoire in one minute, not days. A family can save for the future in stablecoins, safe from local currency collapse. A tourist can move across Africa without worrying about changing currency at every border.

Why Now

The conventional answer is demographics, smartphones, and regulation. Those matter, but they're table stakes. Everyone sees them.

Here's what we saw: the informal economy was already solving this problem, just inefficiently. Hawala networks, bus drivers, family connections, WhatsApp P2P group chats. They work because they're built on trust and local presence, not banking infrastructure.

The insight wasn't "Africa needs better payments." It was "the agent model already exists. It just needs better rails."

Stablecoins gave us those rails. Not because crypto is magic, but because it lets us settle across borders without correspondent banks, without forex desks, without the infrastructure that makes traditional remittance expensive. We're disrupting the middlemen, but retaining the trust. This means we're upgrading the rails people already use.

The regulatory shift matters too, but not in the way most people think. The real change isn't that governments are "embracing crypto." It's that they're tired of watching billions flow through channels they can't see. A compliant stablecoin network that brings informal flows into the light is something regulators actually want.

The Long View

Payments are just the beginning. The real opportunity is intra-African trade.

African countries trade far more with Europe, China, and the US than with each other. Intra-African trade hovers around 15-18% of total trade, compared to roughly 60% for Europe and 40% for Asia. The reasons are structural. Colonial infrastructure built to extract resources rather than connect economies. 54 countries with different regulations, standards, and currencies. Payment systems that until recently made it nearly impossible to settle cross-border transactions in local currencies. A buyer in one African country paying a supplier in another typically had to convert to USD and back again, with fees at each step.

African SMEs have always needed to do business with one another, but the infrastructure has made it nearly impossible. So businesses source locally even when better options exist across the border, or they route payments through Europe or the US, adding days and costs to simple transactions.

The internet connected African businesses. Stablecoins let them actually pay each other.

That's where we're headed. Not just an app for consumers, but rails that any business can build on. Opera is already doing this with MiniPay, using Cashramp's infrastructure to serve users across Africa. We want more of that. Payment apps, neobanks, remittance services, e-commerce platforms. Any product that needs to move money across African borders should be able to plug into our network and go live in days, not years.

The agent network we've built isn't just for Accrue. It's infrastructure that can power an ecosystem. That's the long view: not competing with every payments company on the continent, but becoming the rails they all run on to talk to each other across colonial lines.

Four years ago, I wrote that we started Accrue to solve a problem we had with investing. That was true then. But we kept listening, kept following the data, kept adjusting until something clicked.

Four years later, we're building the rails for how Africa moves money. And we're just getting started.
