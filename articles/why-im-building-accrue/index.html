Why I'm Building Accrue

Africa moves a lot of money. The problem is how. Cross-border payments hit $329 billion in 2025, but 75% moves through informal channels. Here's why we're building the rails for Africa's digital economy.

Fintech Africa Startups

Africa moves a lot of money. The problem is how.

Cross-border payments on the continent hit $329 billion in 2025. Remittances alone account for nearly $100 billion a year, making up over 5% of GDP. But here's the thing: 75% of that money moves through informal channels. Cash in envelopes. Bus drivers carrying bundles across borders. Family networks.

Why? Because formal transfers cost 8% on average, the highest rate in the world. Add currency conversion losses, and you're looking at $5 billion a year vanishing into the friction of moving money.

That's the problem we're solving.

What We Built

Accrue is a mobile app. Anyone in Africa can save in USD stablecoins, send and receive payments instantly, and participate in the global economy. For millions facing volatile local currencies, stablecoins aren't speculation. They're the safest store of value available.

Cashramp is infrastructure. Think M-Pesa on stablecoin rails. It's a stablecoin agent network spread across over 11 African countries. Users walk up to agents, hand over local currency, and receive stablecoin equivalents. When that money needs to cross a border, say from Ghana to Nigeria, the Ghanaian user sends money to an agent in local currency, receives stablecoins at the prevailing market rate, and another agent in Nigeria converts it to naira and deposits it into the recipient's bank account. The user never sees the complexity of cryptocurrency.

Four years of building

We've done this with a small, market-embedded team operating at roughly 10% of the overhead of legacy remittance companies. Western Union took decades to achieve continental coverage. We did it in under 24 months.

The growth isn't hype-driven. It's utility-driven. People use Accrue because it solves real problems.

A trader in Ghana can pay a supplier in Kenya in seconds, not days. A Nigerian freelancer can receive USD from a European client without losing a big chunk to intermediaries. A family can save for the future in stable USD, safe from local currency collapse.

Why Now

Three forces are converging.

First, stablecoin adoption is accelerating. Africa isn't waiting for traditional finance to catch up. Stablecoins offer a way out of currency volatility and a bridge to the global economy.

Second, infrastructure is maturing. Mobile penetration is high. Internet access is expanding. The rails exist. What's missing is the layer that connects them.

Third, regulatory momentum. From Nigeria to Ghana to Kenya's regulatory sandboxes and pan-African trade frameworks, governments are starting to support fintech innovation. South Africa already has a license framework for crypto businesses. Five years ago, governments saw crypto as a threat. Now they're building frameworks for it.

The Longer View

Payments are the wedge. The real opportunity is broader.

Once you're the trusted layer for moving money, you can build everything else on top: credit, insurance, investments, business tools. Every major fintech in emerging markets followed this playbook. We're doing the same, but with stablecoins as the foundation instead of legacy banking infrastructure.
