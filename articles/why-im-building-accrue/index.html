Why I'm Building Accrue

Africa moves a lot of money. The problem is how. Here's why we're building the rails for Africa's digital economy.

Fintech Africa Startups

Cross-border payments on the continent hit $329 billion in 2025. Remittances alone account for nearly $100 billion annually, making up over 5% of GDP in many countries. But here's the thing: 75% of that money moves through informal channels. Cash in envelopes. Bus drivers carrying bundles across borders. Family networks.

Why? Because formal transfers cost 8% on average, the highest rate in the world. Add currency conversion losses, and you're looking at $5 billion a year vanishing into the friction of moving money.

That's the problem we're solving.

What We Built

Accrue is a mobile app. Anyone in Africa can save in USD stablecoins, send and receive payments instantly, and participate in the global economy. For millions facing volatile local currencies, stablecoins aren't speculation. They're the safest store of value available.

Cashramp is infrastructure. Think M-Pesa on stablecoin rails. It's a stablecoin agent network spread across over 11 African countries. Users walk up to agents, hand over local currency, and receive stablecoin equivalents displayed as USD in the app. The conversion is automatic. The user sends local currency, and receives a dollar equivalent, seamlessly.

When that money needs to cross a border, say from Ghana to Nigeria, the Ghanaian user sends money to an agent in local currency, receives stablecoins at the prevailing market rate, and another agent in Nigeria converts it to naira and deposits it into the recipient's bank account. The user never sees the complexity of cryptocurrency. Their local currency simply gains the superpowers of programmable money: a $1 fee, one-minute settlement, and frictionless cross-border movement.

Both products are live today, processing millions of transactions across over 11 African countries. You can check our Wall of Love to see what users are saying. This isn't a pitch deck. It's a business.

Four Years of Work

We've scaled to over 11 African markets. Our payment success rate is 98%, compared to industry averages that often struggle below 80%. Revenue has grown over 15% quarter over quarter, powered mostly by word of mouth.

We've done this with a small, market-embedded team. No bank branches. No compliance teams in every country. Just agents with smartphones and a protocol that handles the rest.

The growth isn't hype-driven. It's utility-driven. People use Accrue because it solves real problems.

A trader in Ghana can pay a supplier in Kenya in one minute, not days. A Nigerian freelancer can receive USD from a European client without losing a big chunk to intermediaries. A family can save for the future in stable USD, safe from local currency collapse.

Why Now

The conventional answer is demographics, smartphones, and regulation. Those matter, but they're table stakes. Everyone sees them.

Here's what we saw: the informal economy was already solving this problem, just inefficiently. Hawala networks, bus drivers, family connections - they work because they're built on trust and local presence, not banking infrastructure. The insight wasn't "Africa needs better payments." It was "the agent model already exists. It just needs better rails."

Stablecoins gave us those rails. Not because crypto is magic, but because it lets us settle across borders without correspondent banks, without forex desks, without the infrastructure that makes traditional remittance expensive. We're not disrupting informal networks. We're upgrading them.

The regulatory shift matters too, but not in the way most people think. The real change isn't that governments are "embracing crypto." It's that they're tired of watching billions flow through channels they can't see. A compliant stablecoin network that brings informal flows into the light is something regulators actually want.

The Longer View

Payments are the wedge. The real opportunity is broader.

But here's why our path is different: we see what banks can't see. Traditional lenders in Africa struggle because they have no data on informal economy participants. No credit history. No bank statements. No way to underwrite.

Our agent network processes their cash flows every week. We know who sends money reliably. We know whose business is growing. We know who pays back the informal credit their agent already extends them. That's an underwriting advantage that only comes from processing millions of transactions at the agent level.

Credit comes first. Then insurance. Then investments. Not because it's the standard fintech playbook, but because each one requires the trust and data that the previous one builds.

The vision: Make moving money between Yaounde and Kampala as easy as moving it between New York and San Francisco.

That's a big goal. But every month, we get closer. And every month, more people depend on us to move their money safely.

That's why I'm building Accrue alongside Clinton & Adesuwa. Not because it's interesting. Because it matters.
