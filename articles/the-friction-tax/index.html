The Friction Tax

Why Intra-African Trade Remains Broken and What It Will Take to Fix It

Trade Fintech Africa
Share on Twitter Share on LinkedIn Share on Bluesky

A garment manufacturer in Accra wants to ship fabric to a buyer in Abidjan. The distance is roughly 500 kilometers. The journey should be straightforward. Instead, it becomes an odyssey.

The manufacturer must prove the fabric qualifies as Ghanaian-origin goods, which requires documentation her customs broker warns might be rejected at the Ivorian border anyway. Her bank needs three weeks to process the letter of credit. The truck will sit at the Elubo crossing for two days while officials argue about inspection protocols. When payment finally arrives, it will have passed through correspondent banks in London and New York, converting from cedis to dollars to euros to CFA francs, losing value at each hop.

By the time the transaction settles, she will have paid what amounts to a 15% tax on doing business with her neighbor.

This is not an exceptional case. This is Tuesday.

The Paradox of Proximity

Africa is a continent of 1.4 billion people sharing borders with each other. Proximity and demand exist in abundance. Yet intra-African trade hovers around 15% of the continent's total trade, depending on how you count.

The standard explanations for this gap tend toward the structural: colonial-era infrastructure designed to extract resources rather than connect neighbors, commodity-dependent economies that compete rather than complement, insufficient industrialization to create complex supply chains.

These explanations are true as far as they go. But they miss something important. They describe why intra-African trade developed the way it did. They do not fully explain why it remains constrained today, even as the political will for integration has never been stronger.

The real answer lies in friction. Not the absence of demand, but the accumulated weight of barriers that make every cross-border transaction more expensive, slower, and less predictable than it should be.

The Friction Stack

Trade friction in Africa operates across multiple layers, each compounding the others. Think of it as a stack, where problems at one level cascade into the next.

At the base sits market access friction. Tariffs still exist across many corridors, and even where preferential rates apply under regional agreements, actually claiming those preferences requires compliance capacity that most small and medium enterprises simply do not have.

Above this sits border and compliance friction. Non-tariff barriers often matter more than tariffs themselves. Duplicated paperwork, inconsistent customs procedures, unpredictable inspections, and informal fees at checkpoints create an environment where crossing a border becomes an exercise in uncertainty.

Then comes logistics friction. Transport costs within Africa remain among the highest in the world relative to distance. A container shipped from China to Mombasa often costs less than moving that same container from Mombasa to Kampala.

Trade finance friction sits higher in the stack. African SMEs face a trade finance gap estimated in the hundreds of billions of dollars. Banks demand collateral that small businesses cannot provide.

Near the top sits the layer that arguably binds everything else together: payments and foreign exchange friction. This is where the garment manufacturer in Accra loses the most. African currencies do not trade directly with each other.

Finally, regulatory fragmentation caps the stack. Fifty-four countries means fifty-four licensing regimes, fifty-four approaches to data protection, fifty-four interpretations of anti-money laundering requirements.

Lessons from Elsewhere

The regions that achieved high intra-regional trade shares did not simply remove tariffs and wait for commerce to flourish. They built infrastructure, literal and institutional, that made cross-border transactions operationally boring.

"Payment infrastructure is necessary but not sufficient. Without parallel progress on logistics, standards, and border facilitation, better payment rails alone cannot unlock trade."
The Latin American Lesson

AfCFTA: Framework Waiting for an Operating System

The African Continental Free Trade Area represents the most ambitious trade integration project the continent has ever attempted. On paper, it creates a single market of 1.4 billion people with a combined GDP exceeding $3 trillion. The agreement has been ratified by most African Union member states. The legal framework exists.

Implementation is another matter. The Guided Trade Initiative has demonstrated both the potential and the limitations. Companies have successfully traded across borders using AfCFTA preferences. But the initiative also revealed how much work remains: incomplete tariff schedules, customs officials unfamiliar with the new procedures, firms struggling to meet origin requirements, and payment settlement that still routes through the old correspondent banking channels.

AfCFTA is the legal architecture. What Africa lacks is the operating system that makes the architecture run. And the most critical component of that operating system is payment infrastructure.

Payments as the Keystone

The Pan-African Payment and Settlement System, PAPSS, represents Africa's most direct attempt to solve the payments layer of the friction stack. Operated by Afreximbank, PAPSS enables cross-border payments in local currencies, settling transactions instantly and eliminating the need for hard currency intermediation.

PAPSS matters because it attacks the right problem. When a Nigerian importer can pay a Ghanaian exporter in naira, with the exporter receiving cedis, without either party bearing correspondent banking fees or multi-day settlement delays, the cost structure of intra-African trade fundamentally shifts.

But PAPSS alone cannot solve the problem. It needs distribution. It needs last-mile infrastructure that connects businesses and individuals to the settlement rail. It needs the agents, the apps, the financial service providers who can translate a sophisticated payment system into something a fabric trader in Kumasi or a produce buyer in Lome can actually use.

The Agent Network Opportunity

Africa's cross-border payment needs are not abstract. They manifest as specific, repeated transactions: a migrant worker in Lagos sending money to family in Accra, a small importer in Nairobi paying a supplier in Kampala, a freelancer in Johannesburg receiving payment from a client in Cairo.

At Accrue, we have watched this opportunity develop from the inside. We started building dollar-cost-averaging investment tools for Africans who wanted exposure to global assets. What we discovered was that our users kept finding ways to use our platform for something we had not anticipated: cross-border payments.

A user in Ghana would fund their Accrue wallet, and a relative in Nigeria would withdraw from theirs. They had invented their own remittance corridor using our savings infrastructure.

This organic behavior told us something important. The demand for cheap, fast, cross-border payments in Africa is so intense that users will hack together solutions from whatever tools are available. Our response was Cashramp: infrastructure that makes this kind of transaction native rather than improvised.

The thesis is straightforward. Intra-African trade is constrained by friction, and the payments layer of that friction is both the most tractable and the most leveraged point of intervention. Solve payments, and you make every other layer of the stack more manageable.

What It Will Take

Fixing intra-African trade is not a single-variable problem. Payment infrastructure is necessary but not sufficient, as the Latin American experience demonstrates. The full solution requires progress across every layer of the friction stack.

The Boring Trade Goal

The ultimate measure of success for intra-African trade integration is not a percentage point increase in official statistics. It is the garment manufacturer in Accra who ships fabric to Abidjan without thinking about it.

The transaction settles in hours, not weeks. The payment costs basis points, not percentages. The documentation flows digitally. The truck crosses the border on schedule. The buyer and seller focus on their actual business rather than on navigating the bureaucratic obstacle course.

Europe achieved this. Trading across borders within the EU feels domestic because the infrastructure makes it domestic. Asia is building toward this through interoperability rather than unification. Africa has the frameworks in place. AfCFTA provides the legal architecture. PAPSS provides a payment backbone.

What remains is building the connective tissue: the agent networks, the digital platforms, the last-mile infrastructure that translates continental ambitions into daily transactions.

"The friction is real. But so is the opportunity to eliminate it."

This is not a problem that will be solved by governments alone, or by private sector actors alone. It requires both. Policy frameworks that enable innovation. Companies willing to build infrastructure in markets that larger players ignore. Patient capital that understands the compounding returns of reduced friction.

The prize is substantial. Estimates suggest that fully implementing AfCFTA could increase intra-African trade by over 50% within a decade. That translates to hundreds of billions of dollars in additional commerce, millions of jobs, and a fundamental restructuring of how African economies relate to each other and to the world.
