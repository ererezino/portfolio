Obada Baddest

A tribute to my dad, Obada.

Share on Twitter Share on Facebook Share on LinkedIn Share on Bluesky Share on Threads

My brother, Ogaga, has a thing for giving everyone in the house nicknames. But nothing could have prepared me for coming home over the holidays one year and hearing him call our dad, Obada, a whole grown man, Obada Baddest. I nearly lost it. But everyone else in the house just carried on like it was the most normal thing in the world. They'd heard it before. I hadn't. And it was the funniest, most perfectly ridiculous thing.

My dad was one of the best people I've ever known. Not in the way people say that loosely about someone after they're gone. I mean it in the way you mean something you've watched with your own eyes for your entire life. He was kind. He was loving. He cared for the people around him, and even on his dying bed, he was still finding ways to help others in need. That was just who he was. He couldn't turn it off.

My last real conversation with my dad was about his music.

Yes, among everything else, my father also happened to be an upcoming gospel artist with unreleased tracks. A whole side quest none of us saw coming.

I'd asked him what he wanted for his birthday, and he told me he'd like to finally publish his book and release some of his songs. Now, there's something they say about never ruling out that your man could one day decide to become an artist. I'd always known my dad liked composing gospel songs, but I never knew he actually dreamed of recording them.

So I asked him, which songs?

He looked me dead in the eyes, completely serious, and said: "The popular ones."

Then he rattled off the names of about three tracks like he was giving me a tracklist for a debut album.

I almost laughed my belly out. I said, popular where? Who knows your songs, please?

And without missing a beat, he told me that in his church, his songs were bangers.

That was my dad. Completely serious and effortlessly hilarious at the same time. We had banter like that a lot, the kind of back-and-forth that only comes from a place of deep love and mutual respect.

I'll miss him more than I know how to say. He made sacrifices I'll probably never fully understand. He worked harder than anyone I've known to make sure my family had everything we needed. And he didn't just talk about how the world should be. He lived it. Every single day. He walked his talk.

Thank you, Daddy. I love you.

🤍

Forever, your biggest fan. Even bigger than the church congregation.
